Australia's Job Vacancy Trends and Analysis

My visualization dashboard aims to provide a comprehensive overview of job vacancy trends across Australia . By analyzing data from various states and industries, the dashboard offers insights into how employment opportunities have evolved. This tool is designed to help users better understand the current Australian job market, highlighting which sectors are growing and where potential skill shortages may exist. Through this analysis, viewers can gain a clearer picture of the nation's employment landscape and make informed decisions based on these trends.

Trend of Job Vacancies from Nov 2009 to Aug 2024

From November 2009 to November 2019, job vacancies in Australia showed a steady upward trend, growing from 148,900 to 230,500. This period reflects a stable and gradually improving labor market, likely driven by consistent economic growth. However, in early 2020, the onset of the COVID-19 pandemic caused a sharp decline in job vacancies, dropping to 128,400 in May 2020 from 227,400 in February 2020. The pandemic significantly disrupted the labor market, with many businesses reducing hiring or shutting down altogether.

Following this dip, there was a rapid recovery, with job vacancies peaking at 473,100 in May 2022. This surge reflected strong demand for labor as businesses resumed activities, often to meet post-pandemic demand or compensate for previous disruptions. Interestingly, from late 2022 onward, job vacancies began to decline gradually, reaching 329,900 in August 2024. This recent trend may indicate a normalization of the labor market, with hiring levels adjusting after the peaks of 2021-2022. Despite the decline, the current figures still reflect a stronger job market compared to pre-pandemic levels.

Tracking Job Vacancies Across Australian States: Aug 2019 - Aug 2024

Job vacancies, during this period, across Australian states and territories remained relatively stable, with New South Wales and Victoria consistently leading in the number of vacancies. However, the onset of the COVID-19 pandemic in early 2020 led to a significant drop, with South Wales vacancies plummeting from 79,300 in February 2020 to 39,800 by May 2020, and similar trends seen across other states. This dramatic decline was a result of widespread lockdowns and economic disruptions, causing a sharp contraction in the job market.

The recovery phase began by August 2020, as job vacancies started rebounding. By May 2021, the figures had surged, with New South Wales reaching 112,800 and Victoria at 87,600, reflecting an economy regaining strength. Throughout 2022, job vacancies continued to climb, peaking around mid-2022, likely due to strong economic activity and pent-up demand after prolonged restrictions. However, from late 2022 onwards, a gradual decline set in, as reflected by reduced figures in 2023 and 2024. For instance, by August 2024, New South Wales had decreased to 101,400, signaling a possible normalization after earlier peaks. This recent trend suggests stabilization in hiring, with vacancies adjusting as businesses balance post-pandemic recovery with more cautious growth strategies.

Tracking Job Vacancies Across Industries

The data highlights how job vacancies across various industries in Australia have changed compared to pre-COVID-19 levels. Some sectors experienced significant growth, such as Arts and Recreation Services (up 158.1%), Accommodation and Food Services (up 114.9%), and Health Care and Social Assistance (up 104.6%). This suggests a strong rebound in sectors that were heavily impacted by the pandemic, particularly those reliant on public interaction and essential services. However, some industries like Administrative and Support Services (-18.5%) and Financial and Insurance Services (-7.2%) saw declines, indicating a slower recovery or a shift in market demand.

As of 2024, Health Care and Social Assistance leads in job vacancies, making up 18.3% of the total, reflecting the continued demand for healthcare services. Other prominent sectors include Professional, Scientific, and Technical Services (11.7%) and Accommodation and Food Services (9.5%). This distribution showcases the ongoing demand in critical and service-oriented industries, as well as a notable presence in fields that support economic infrastructure and daily life needs.
